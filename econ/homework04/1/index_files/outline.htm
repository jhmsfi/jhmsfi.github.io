The Basic Bike Race Model
At each step, agents move forward one space or do not move on a 100 space vector.
Agents have a strategy for moving in four possible states: with someone in front or no one in front and with someone in back or no one in back.  There are 16 possible strategies.
Agents have a total amount of energy (150) and pay a cost each time they move.
The largest cost is paid by agents with no one in adjoining spaces (3), the second largest cost is paid by a leader with no one in front of them (2), and the smallest cost is paid by a follower (1).
Key Assumptions
Strategies are time-step invariant.
Agents do not adapt to other agents’ strategies and may continue unsuccessful strategies.
There is no limit to the number of agents on each space.
There are no dynamics associated with passing or sharing a space.
Initial Results
When all strategies are played by at least one agent, the most successful strategy is “move unless someone is behind you and no one is in front of you.”
The four most successful strategies all include moving when someone is in front of you, no matter who is behind you.
When only successful strategies are used by all the agents, there is a universal tie with all agents moving in each round.
Limitations
Unrealistic: If strategies are determined randomly, many agents do not move at all.
Ties: Many agents can win the race, especially those with similar strategies.
One Space at a Time: There is no way to jump ahead of another rider.
3 speed case
We observe inertia problem in the two move case. Where strategy space for each state is {0,1}.
Add one strategy. {0,1,2} corresponding to {slow down, keep pace, accelerate}.
Cost function  is zero if slow down, {-2.1,-2,-1,-1} if keep pace, twice the amount if accelerate and additional cost -0.5 for acceleration.
Position change 2 if accelerated.
Tie breaking rule ( The one with more energy left).
State dependant Aggressiveness (Run=500)
Separate leaders tend to slow down.
Group leaders are slightly aggressive (cost low).
Group followers have dubious strategies.
Follower strategies are more volatile, less clear.
Energy Limited Case  E=120
When preserving energy is important, there is clear change of pattern.
Aggressiveness is monotonic w.r.t. position.
Winning players are more aggressive followers.
Leaders have more incentive to preserve energy.
No clear dominated strategy
In the E=150 case; 76 out of the 81 possible strategy ends up in the winning pool. E=120 case; 68 out of 81 strategy in the winning pool.
So, in a non-dynamic game, there is no clearly dominant or dominated strategy due to the large number of players.
At later time-steps, however, agents that are clearly behind have no reason to slow down.
In the second case, when resources are limited, it is advantageous to be slightly aggressive while following and less aggressive while leading.
Winner Characteristics
Tie problem
Allowing ties reduces strategic interplay.
The negative externalities of the players’ strategies are low.  My strategic choice does not change your payoff.
We would imagine it to be different once we line them up and allow only one person to occupy one position.
Budgeting problem
Without forward looking w.r.t. budget constraint, most of the agents are over aggressive. We should include more rational adaptation by agents.
Agents should avoid leading if they do not have enough energy left.
Planned Extensions
Make losing agents adaptive:
If more than 2 spaces behind, mutate strategies or adopt the leader’s strategy.
Model end-game with backward induction.
Model benefits as capacities to move more than one step forward and model the amount moved each round stochastically.
Include spatial dynamics and costs of passing with movement on a lattice.
Next Step GA
Agents who died in one round, should receive a mutated strategy in the next round.
Simultaneously run many rounds and calculate overall fitness for different strategies.
Strategies should be round-variant.
Winning strategies propagate.
Evolve the system: have best strategies play against each other.
Potential Applications
Technological Development: Both leaders and second-movers have advantages but innovations cannot be too far ahead or behind the pack.
Delegate Competition in Party Nominations: Candidates gather delegate endorsements but become target if identified as front-runner.
Key dynamics of the social phenomenon would have to be movement in packs, winner-take-all outcomes, and competitive disadvantages from being the leader by a small margin.
