Workplace Drama: How do Organizations Deal with Ego

Motivation

What happens when you have a conflict with your boss? Fight, or step back? This is a hard choice for most employees. Although a victory may seem attractive, open complaints come with high risks. Many complaints end with ruthless retaliation from the targeted managers, often in the form of deprived opportunities within organizations, the end of promotion, or even employment termination.

Complaining is not only meaningful for employees. From the management perspective, complaint is an important channel for information flow, which helps keep employees’ loyalty and well-being on a reasonable level (Dehue et al., 2012). Previous literature often treated the complaint behavior as the outcome (Heck et al., 2005; Yang, 2020) or the predictor (Aube & Rousseau, 2016) of cultural, psychological and organizational factors, aiming to help the management team keep complaints on an “optimal” level that keeps information transparency and administrative efficiency simultaneously.

However, complaint behaviors, like any complex social and psychological process, are context-embedded and dynamic. The specific personality, social positions and local network structures of the related parties can decide the process and result of a complaint event; and the organizational culture evolves over time that very similar behavior could receive fundamentally different treatments under different circumstances. To briefly summarize, the social dynamics of workplace complaints reside between the micro-level of power relations and the macro-level of the organizational environment, and can only be understood there.

        In this project, we use a simulation model to trace this interesting dynamic. We assume a hierarchical structure of the organization by modeling them as binary trees, and set the most important psychological factors in complaint behaviors (i.e., ego, well-being and reputation) as parameters that co-evolve with social decisions of agents. The results of our work show that, when varying the firm-wide ego, four phase transitions occur when cross-sectioned with organizational size. Only larger organizations can support larger-ego employees and pro-manager policies.

Rules of the Game

        Our model is set up as a virtual world where individuals and the environment interact over thousands of time steps. In each round of the simulation, the organizational culture and structure (including hierarchical depth, probability of having an issue to report, pro-employee or pro-manager culture and average ego) are pre-determined, while individual decisions, well-being and reputation change over time.

  • For each simulation round, we first initialize the fixed organizational environment by:
  • modeling a company as a binary tree of depth 2*d, member egos ego_c, complaint probability complaint_prob, and organizational culture L.
  • initialize member well-being w as 80, member reputation r as 100, and perceived successful rate of open complaint p_t as 0.5. These parameters change over time.
  • Ego_c, 2*d, complaint_prob, L, and p_t are organizational level parameters, while w and r belong to individuals.
  • For each time step t within a simulation round:
  • Complaint needs of  complaint_prob are assigned to random members.
  • Employee Moves:
  • If well-being w < ego_c, they may file a complaint, depending on the prior acceptance probability.
  • If the perceived success rate is too low, seek private resolution. Otherwise, go to an official complaint.
  • Manager moves:
  • Top manager (the judge) moves: For official complaint, weighs employee well-being against reputation change. If delta well-being < reputation change delta(r), accept the claim.
  • Targeted manager moves: for a private resolution, accept the claim if the ego is not too high.
  • Outcomes of Individual Actions:
  • For an official complaint, if the (higher) manager helps the employee, employee well-being returns to 100, and the targeted manager is penalized in reputation. If the manager helps the lower manager, the employee's well-being decreases due to retaliation.
  • For a private resolution, if the manager accepts the suggestion, reputation increases as an honor credit.
  • Probability of Ruin: if one employee's well-being falls to 0, there will be a lawsuit.
  • Keeping records of parameter change of each time step.
  • Steady state average based on Monte Carlo methods.

Research Questions

  • RQ1 Ego: How does the distribution of ego influence acceptance probability, along with the well-being and reputation of a firm?
  • RQ2 Organizational Culture: How does changing the manager-focused vs employee-focused vs balanced decision functions impact acceptance probability, along with the well-being and reputation of a firm?
  • RQ3 Power: How does changing absolute vs relative reporting impact acceptance probability, along with the well-being and reputation of a firm?
  • RQ4 Topology: How does the width and depth of the organization impact acceptance probability, along with the well-being and reputation of a firm?

Results

Figure 2 shows a sample of a parametrized Monte-Carlo run. In this case, well-being oscillates from its initial position of 80. Open complaints are resolved quickly, and acceptance rate soon jumps to 100 after 34 epochs.

Figure 2: Averaged Monte-Carlo results (n=1000)  of setting complaint rate to 0.13 and binary tree depth to 8 (2^8 =256 nodes), with low ego (EGO = 30). Oscillatory behavior happens with reputation steadily declining, indicating preference to make official reports (over private resolution).

Ego.

Figures 3, 4, and 5 show the impact of varying ego on acceptance probability, well-being, and reputation, across organizations of different size. Immediately, when varying ego across acceptance probability, we notice four phase transitions. For ego, a strong phase transition occurs between 49 and 50, which is marked by a large amount of instability (as seen by the explosive variance).

However, the depth of organization influences the effects. For small organizations with less than 256 agents, acceptance probability falls to zero. For large organizations (n>1023), the effects are muted with probabilities over 80%. Of great interest are organizations between d=8 and 10, where a region of instability emerges. Then, if ego > 80, no depth can save their employees.

Figure 3: Acceptance probability when varying ego (1-100) and depth (4-14).

A similar story can be seen with wellbeing. In particular, we see the blacked out region in the well-being variance denote deterministic convergence. However, for mid-sized organizations, there is great instability.

Figure 4: Well-being when varying ego (1-100) and depth (4-14).

The reason for the acceptance probability’s fluctuation may be explained by reputation effects . Reputation undergoes significant instability precisely at the 49-50 transition for ego. From 0-49, reputation steadily declines, indicating favorable conditions for employees. However, due to instability induced at ego = 50, the organizational ecosystem shifts to favor managers, culminating at ego = 80.

Figure 5: Reputation when varying ego (1-100) and depth (4-14).

Together, this allows us to tune into the region of greatest sensitivity– with organizations of depth 8 to 9 (256 to 512),  and ego range between 59 and 80. In summary, we find acceptance probability to be a complex relationship between ego and organizational depth.

Structural effects.

Next, we consider the influence of structural effects, namely the endogenous complaint probability and depth, in a low ego environment (ego = 30). In terms of well-being and reputation, its impact is minimal. Average well-being is slightly higher in smaller organizations, all things equal. The same can be said with reputation.

However, when we moved to the region of instability, the story changes. As shown in figure 8 and 9, organizations are viable only when a) organizations are small and the complaint probability is low (<0.04). Or if the depth of the organization is sufficiently large (d>=8). Once again, we observe 8 to 9 in depth becoming a critical turning point for organizations with a binary tree structure.

In sum, when ego is low, complaint probabilty affects organization well-being linearly. However, complaints are ultimately resolved. However, when ego is mid-high, then regions of instability emerge. Small firms cannot sustain complaint rates, whereas larger firms can amortize well-being over its size.

Conclusion

  • We study the impact of ego, firm size, and company culture on the dynamics of reporting manager misbehavior.
  • When varying the firm-wide ego, four phase transitions occur when cross-sectioned with organizational size (depth).
  • Larger organizations can support employees with larger egos.
  • This may be attributed to direct effects on reputation– when the top-manager begins ruling in favor of managers due to employee’s having too high an ego.
  • In regions of stability, varying the complaint rate linearly decreases well-being. However, in regions of instability, a similar phase-transitions between mid to large size organizations.
  • Lastly, organizational culture influences well-being (directly related to report and acceptance). Pro-Employees and neutral settings sustain the organization.
  • For pro-manager policies, only large organizations can sustain it. Results show an increase in private resolution, thus allowing the co-evolution of reputation and well-being.
