Overview

In this project, we were tasked with modeling how a society changes a pre-existing norm. Norm change and social diffusion have been extensively studied across computational social science [1], with applications across several fields including the adoption of new technologies [2], adherence to novel health measures such as masks during pandemics [3], as well as the gradual shift in public opinion about contentious issues such as same-sex marriage and abortion over time [4, 5]. Neither of us had previous experience with such modeling approaches before, making this project especially interesting (and challenging)! Nonetheless, we learned a lot and — dare we say — “normalized” the idea of using such models in our own work.

What is normal?

“Normal” is a loaded term with varying meanings. It can refer to “prescriptively normal” i.e., “ideal” or “desirable” beliefs and behaviors [6]; often with real-world adverse consequences for those who fail to conform to such “normal” perceptions and practices. On the other hand, “descriptive” accounts of normalcy incorporate statistical considerations, with “normal” being the “average” or the “most frequent” [6]. We sought to capture both descriptive and prescriptive notions of normalcy in our model. In our context, “descriptive norms” referred to the majority or average norms held by our simulated population and “prescriptive norms” referred to “ideal” norms originating from an external “shock” driven by highly influential individuals.

Approach and assumptions

To approach the modeling of social norm transition, we utilize agent-based modeling (ABM). Agent-based models are simulations of the interactions between individual actors or “agents”. Agents possess certain attributes and their interactions follow pre-defined rules that are modeled over multiple iterations. Agent-based models are commonly used to capture the diffusion of new technologies and the spread of contagious diseases [2, 7]. Thus, they may not be entirely appropriate for our context yet they offer valuable insights for initial investigations.

We designed an agent-based model using the MESA framework in Python [8] with these assumptions:

  • Constant population: an unchanging number of individuals in our universe
  • Constant social network structure: any given individual has an unchanging number of “neighbors” i.e., individuals with whom they are “connected” - there is no emergence or dissolution of links

Individual agents possessed these attributes:

  • Demographic features:
  • Age (ordinal variable with 4 buckets): Gen Z, Millennials, Gen X, Boomers
  • Socioeconomic status (ordinal variable with 4 buckets): Low, Medium, High, Very High
  • Gender (categorical variable with 3 buckets): Non-binary, Female, Male
  • Norm belief: a binary variable indicating opposition (0) or support (1) for a norm
  • Openness to change or influenceability: a continuous variable ranging from 0 to 1. High values meant that individuals were more likely to modify their norm.
  • Popularity or influentiality: a continuous variable ranging from 0 to 1. High values meant that individuals were more likely to modify their neighbors’ norm beliefs (see below for more details).

We initialized the model with the following parameters defined on global, system level:

  • Homophily threshold:

In order to approximate real world social networks where similar individuals tend to cluster together, we used demographics (age, socioeconomic status, and gender) to calculate homophily — how similar or dissimilar any two given individuals are. The overall homophily was calculated using the Jaccard distance between two agents’ vectors of socio-demographic attributes. It was a continuous variable ranging from 0 to 1 where higher values indicate larger distance between individuals, i.e. less similarity between them. We varied the model with these homophily values: [0, 0.25, 0.33, 0.5, 0.66, 1].The higher value threshold would yield a more homophilous network where people have a limited connectivity with different others in terms of socio-demographic attributes.

  • Prevalence of pre-existing norm:

We varied the model with these proportions of individuals initially opposing and supporting a particular norm: [0.1, 0.9], [0.25, 0.75], [0.4, 0.6], [0.5, 0.5].

  • Distribution of openness to change or influenceability within the population:

We varied the model with influenceability values randomly drawn from these distributions: [normal, exponential growth, power-law, bimodal, uniform].  

  • Distribution of popularity or influentiality within population:

We varied the model with influentiality values drawn from these distributions: [power-law, uniformly distributed].The power law distribution approximates the real world in which you have a small number of highly popular individuals. The uniform distribution models a more egalitarian society as well as disables the popularity parameter – it means as if modeling without this parameter altogether.

Weighted Influence of Neighbors’s Norms:

where ΣPN=0  is the sum of the popularities (i.e., influentialities) of an agent’s neighbors who oppose the norm, ΣPN=1  is the sum of the popularities (i.e., influenceabilities) of an agent’s neighbors who support the norm, and ΣP is the sum of the popularities (i.e., influenceabilities) of an agent’s neighbors.

The rules are (in sequential order of implementation):

  • If the weighted influence of an agent’s neighbors’ norms are <=0.5, then there won’t be any change in the agent’s norm.
  • If the weighted influence of an agent’s neighbors’ norms are >= 0.75, then the agent’s norm will change to correspond to that of their neighbors’.
  • If the weighted influence of an agent’s neighbors’ norms are less than 0.75 but greater than 0.5, the agent’s own openness to change comes into the picture:
  • If the agent is highly open to change (>= 0.75), they will change their norm to conform with the majority of their direct neighbors.
  • If the agent isn’t open to change (<= 0.75), they won’t change their norm irrespective of prevalence of norm in the direct social network neighborhood.
  • If the agent is of intermediate openness to change (0.25 < O < 0.75), there will be a 50% chance of changing their belief and a 50% chance of not changing their norm.

Interpreting the results

When there’s no homophily at all, we have a very dense, almost fully connected, random network. As a consequence, it is difficult to reach consensus because any small change in the network leads to multiple downstream effects, thus keeping the network in a constant state of flux. The somewhat connected network (homophily = 0.25) seems to be optimal, with participants not being too similar to each other (this prevents excessive density) but at the same time, the network not being excessively connected.  As homophily increases (i.e., people connect only with similar others), it becomes more difficult to reach convergence and it also takes more steps to reach convergence. This is probably because the network becomes sparse, with highly similar participants clustering their own “bubbles” or “echo chambers”. This may be akin to highly segregated societies (e.g., based on race, ethnicity, gender, class, caste or tribe) in the real world. In summary, we conclude that changing a pre-existing social norm is difficult (rarely occurring and taking more time) in highly homophilous networks because there are fewer connections with people different from oneself.

Interpreting the results

As a certain norm becomes more prevalent in the society, it becomes easier and faster to reach convergence and it also takes more steps to reach convergence. Conversely, it is the most difficult to reach convergence when the distribution of the norm is more balanced (0.5,  0.5). This may be akin to highly polarized real-world societies where half the population believes in one thing, and the other half believes in something else. A pessimistic interpretation of this is akin to the authoritarian critique of deliberative democracies, viz., consensus is hard to achieve and is achieved very slowly, thus leading to policy paralysis. A homogenous society where people share similar beliefs may be better for unanimous, quick decision-making. On the flip side, even in the highly polarized society, consensus is almost always reached in our model. The only tradeoff is the higher amount of time taken for this to occur.

Interpreting the results

Drawing from a normal distribution, exponential growth distribution, and a uniform distribution, we observe simulations that all lead to fast convergence. The normal distribution seems to be the most efficient in terms of reaching consensus, perhaps because of the high proportion of medium influencability individuals. Such individuals aren’t too stubborn to be stuck in their ways, but aren’t too open-minded to be continuously changing their minds, thus leading to an “ideal” mixture of individuals for the purposes of reaching consensus. The uniform distribution performs similar to the normal distribution, but takes slightly longer. This is likely because of the higher number of people at the extremes. The problem of extreme individuals is exemplified in the bimodal distribution that takes the longest to converge, and doesn’t converge one-third of the time. With exponential growth, there are too many highly influenced individuals, who keep switching their beliefs, thus preventing norm convergence. On the flip side, with logarithmic decay, there are mostly low to medium influenceability individuals. Such individuals change their minds in a suitable environment, but don’t constantly flip to different norms. This also allows for fast convergence.

Interpreting the results

Both models perform relatively similarly, and are able to induce norm change even in the most “difficult” situations previously identified. There are certain edge cases with the distribution of popularity that follows the power-law when consensus is not reached: this is very likely due to the presence of several highly influential individuals within one’s direct neighborhood that sometimes leads to local fluctuations.

Introducing an intervention

Until now, change in an individual’s norm belief depended solely on their own direct social network neighborhood. Now, we assume a completely different scenario. Imagine there is a very strong pre-existing social norm the vast-majority of the population adheres to. Now, due to an external shock, the “government” decides to “hire” the influencers to propagate the opposite of the existing social norm. The question arises: how many “most influential people” do you need to hire to overtake the existing social norm?

In this setup, we keep the last model specification (the most “difficult” scenarios):

  • Relatively high level of homophily: 0.5.
  • Bimodal distribution of openness: lots of people with very little or very high openness and nothing in between.
  • Popularity is drawn from power-law distribution.

Initially, everyone has the same social norm (let’s say, value 0). Then, the “government” intervenes and hires a certain share of “most influential people” starting from the most influential to the least influential to have the opposite norm, viz. — 1. We experiment with the share of people hired while keeping other parameters constant. We measure how many times the population reached a consensus, number of steps it took, on average, as well as the share of times the society reached the “desired” consensus converging to the norm of 1.

Interpreting the results

Given that the highly influential individuals have to completely flip the pre-existing social norm, a higher proportion of such influential individuals is needed for the norm to change. Even so, overall norm convergence is rarely achieved. Furthemore, the “prescriptive” norm is achieved at an even lower rate. This illustrates how hard it is to change pre-existing majority social norms, even with the presence of highly influential individuals. A pessimistic interpretation would highlight how difficult it is to achieve a new norm if it didn’t exist before (e.g., widespread mask-wearing in the US during the COVID-19 pandemic). The highly influential individuals help but are not enough for norm change. Thus, even when influential entities such as celebrities and institutions (e.g., Taylor Swift or the CDC) pushed for a norm change, the efforts weren’t enough. Our model doesn’t include any punishment or social cost for not following the norms prescribed by the government and promoted by influential individuals or organizations, and thus future implementations should incorporate such incentives to ensure higher norm compliance. On the flip side, the model results are somewhat encouraging given that even a high number of influential individuals can’t really shift a widespread pre-existing norm. This model demonstrates the constraints for an authoritarian or dictatorial system where highly influential individuals/institutions push for particular norms that fit their own prescriptive moral framework yet they still fail in a context of absence of explicit punishment.    

“Easy” and “hard” norm transitions

By varying various parameters such as homophily, prior distribution of norms, openness to change (or influenceability), popularity (or influentiality), and the role of an external “shock”, our model provided a sense of what norm changes are easy or hard to achieve. Norm change is difficult to achieve in highly socially homophilous or heterophilous contexts. An intermediate sweet spot is needed where people don’t live in echo chambers but there is still a relatively sparse network. Not surprisingly, norm change is also the hardest to achieve in a highly polarized society (with respect to the pre-existing distribution of norms), and the easiest in a homogenous society where people already think the same. In terms of openness to change, a society with only extremely stubborn and extremely influenceable individuals is hard to change — the stubborn individuals don’t budge whereas the influenceable individuals keep changing their minds and don’t stabilize. A society with moderately influenceable individuals seems to be the easiest for norm change to occur. Highly influential individuals can indeed induce norm convergence in society relatively easily, however if the pre-existing norms are extremely widespread, even highly influential individuals struggle to change norms in the absence of other incentives such as punishment or social cost.

Reality check

While this model of course is only a first step towards modeling a more comprehensive understanding of social norm transition, we aim to present real-world examples of social norm dynamics that could inspire and guide further refinement of the model.

To do so, we source survey data from a nationally representative random-probability survey, namely, the LISS (Longitudinal Internet Studies for the Social Sciences) panel administered by Centerdata (Tilburg University, The Netherlands) [9]. Among the various studies available, we focused on two waves of surveys conducted in 2017 and 2019 that asked people about their social norms and values. One particular question we analyzed was: “Can abortion always be justified, never be justified, or something in between?” Respondents could answer on a scale from 1 to 10, where 1 indicates that abortion can never be justified and 10 indicates that it can always be justified.

Figure 3 illustrates the distribution of support for abortion in the years 2017 vs 2019. The x-axis represents the degree of support for abortion on a scale from 1 to 10, where 1 indicates that abortion should never be justified, and 10 indicates that it should always be justified. The y-axis shows the number of respondents. The number of respondents who selected each level of support varies significantly. In 2017, the lowest levels of support (1 and 2) had relatively few respondents, with fewer than 20 people each. As the level of support increases, the number of respondents generally increases. The highest level of support (10) has the most respondents, with over 80 individuals indicating that abortion should always be justified. In 2019, while strong support for abortion remained high, there was a more even distribution between the highest levels of support (8 and 10) in 2019, and a slight increase in respondents at the lowest levels of support.

Conclusion

Our agent-based model successfully captured how a society changes a pre-existing norm by incorporating parameters such as homophily, prior distribution of norms, openness to change (or influenceability), popularity (or influentiality), and the role of an external “shock” in which there is a sudden need to overturn the existing social norm. Playing around with these parameters also gave us an insight into the contexts where norm change is easy or hard. We were able to capture “descriptive norms” — based on the majority or average norms held in the populations — as well as “prescriptive norms” originating from an external “shock” initiated by “government ” and further propagated by highly influential individuals in the social network. We also discussed our model insights alongside a real-world instance of norm-change, viz., abortion.

Most certainly, there are many limitations in our model that can be addressed in future implementations. These limitations and corresponding suggested future directions are:

  • Constant population and constant social network structure: real world societies have changing populations as well as changing social structures (e.g., some friends fall out of touch whereas new social groups form over time). Future work could incorporate fluctuating population sizes and rewiring of the network to incorporate these factors.
  • Homophily based on demographics (age, gender, socioeconomic status): we currently only have 3 categories of demographics. In turn, these categories only have a few buckets each. Future work could add other factors (e.g., educational level), make these values continuous instead of categorical, and also include explicit assumptions about the contribution of each variable to opinion change — for example, openness to changing one’s norm may decrease with age and increase with higher levels of education.
  • Prior distribution of norms: currently our model incorporates norm belief as a binary (support/oppose). However, real world views tend to be more nuanced, and thus future implementations could incorporate a more granular or continuous instantiation of norm belief in individuals.
  • We could also add other factors such as presence of explicit punishment, (perceived) costs of transition, or other incentives that may foster norm change.
